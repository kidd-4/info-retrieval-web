class="subhead">Negotiations Ongoing for Part-Time Faculty </h2>\n\t\n\t<p class="byline"><span class="section-badge">News</span> by <a href="/author/danielle-rudnicka-lavoie">Danielle Rudnicka-Lavoie</a> \u2014 Published September 21, 2015 | <em class="ss-icon ss-chat"></em><a class="comment-count" data-disqus-identifier="thelink_entry_7406" href="#disqus_thread">Comments</a></p>\n\t\n\t<p><a class="twitter-follow-button" data-dnt="true" data-show-count="false" data-show-screen-name="true" href="https://twitter.com/rl_danielle">Follow @rl_danielle</a></p>\n\t\n\t<div class="entry-images full flexslider"><ul class="slides group">\n\t\n\t\t<li class="figure">\n\t\t\t<a class="cb" href="https://thelinknewspaper.ca/images/made/images/articles/Volume_36/News/_resized/5CA.CUPFA(WillieWilson)_900_600_90.jpg" rel="images" title="Patrice Blais is CUPFA\u2019s Vice-President Collective Agreement and Grievance.">\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t<img alt="" src="/images/made/images/articles/Volume_36/News/_resized/5CA.CUPFA(WillieWilson)_820_547_90.jpg"/>\n\t\t\t</a>\n\t\t\t<p>Patrice Blais is CUPFA\u2019s Vice-President Collective Agreement and Grievance. Photo Willie Wilson</p>\n\t\t</li>\n\t</ul></div>\n\t\n\t\n\t\t<div class="intro"><p>The Concordia University Part-Time Faculty Association (<span class="caps">CUPFA</span>) has been without a collective agreement for 144 days, according to a clock counting up the minutes on its website Tuesday morning. </p></div>\n\t\n\n\t\n\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\t<p>The union\u2019s last agreement, which expired on April 30\u2014and was only officially signed on March 17\u2014has not yet been replaced with another contract, despite negotiations being held three times per month since the end of August.</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cWe\u2019re trying to get things moving,\u201d said Patrice Blais, Vice President of Collective Agreement and Grievance at <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> in an interview with <em>The Link</em>. </p>\n\n\t<p>After the collective agreement expired at the end of August 2012, negotiations started right away for the 2012-2015 agreement. According to Blais, the approach towards labour was not proactive because there was a lack of academics on the negotiating team, as well as an aggressive attitude towards negotiation from both sides.</p>\n\n\t<p>A change of tone in the negotiations was necessary to move forward, he said. \u201cPeople were not in solution mode.\u201d </p>\n\n\t<p>The negotiations of the 2012-2015 collective agreement only made headway in the fall of 2014, when <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> had to make a decision to finalize negotiations up until that point. Their most recent agreement was reached in principle on Sept. 28, 2014, according to a statement released by the university. </p>\n\n\t<p>Regardless of the fact that Concordia\u2019s administration wanted to make a longer deal, the <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> negotiating team did not want to agree to anything past the fiscal year of 2015. This ended with a retroactive three-year agreement that was finalized just a month before its expiration. <br/>\nBlais called this an \u201cinterim agreement.\u201d</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cThe university has very little money to bring to the table,\u201d Blais acknowledged. \u201cIt\u2019s about getting part-time faculty more involved in research, by having them more active on committees and university bodies\u2014also understanding that the university will have limits on how much money they want to bring to the table.</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cWhat we want to get is work, respect and recognition. Those are the priorities of our negotiations.\u201d</p>\n\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t<blockquote class="pullquote">\n\t\t\t<p>\u201cWhat we want to get is work, respect and recognition. Those are the priorities of our negotiations,\u201d said Patrice Blais. </p>\n\t\t</blockquote>\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\t<p>Currently, part-time faculty members make $7,865 per three-credit course, plus an eight per cent vacation pay\u2014a pay rate that is generalized to all part-time faculty, regardless of discipline or seniority. The university may assign courses to the same professor multiple years in a row to lessen their class preparation workload, but this is not always possible.</p>\n\n\t<p>Budget cuts in education and the public sector from the provincial government have made the current negotiations arduous. Concordia has lost $30 million since December 2012, according to its former Chief Financial Officer Patrick Kelly. Part-time faculty members have been the hardest hit by these cuts, suffering a seven per cent decrease in the number of courses taught in the past year, which represents about sixty jobs lost, Blais said. \u201cOur members are worried about this. They\u2019re the victims of the academic cuts being made,\u201d he said.</p>\n\n\t<p>With no suggestion that student enrolment at Concordia is decreasing, some classes will be canceled and fewer sections will be offered in existing courses\u2014with a higher reliance on online courses.</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cThese are not good times when you\u2019re cutting left, right and centre. Our slogan is that \u2018Our working conditions are your learning conditions,\u2019 which is the message that we like to send to our students,\u201d said Blais.</p>\n\n\t<p>The effects of this are unknown for the moment but the most concrete results of this are class overcrowding as well as a drop in the quality of how a class can feasibly be taught. </p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cIf courses are cancelled, students suffer,\u201d stated Ted Stathopoulos, President of the Concordia University Faculty Association (<span class="caps">CUFA</span>), which represents full-time faculty.</p>\n\n\t<p>The situation is worse in certain departments than others, according to Blais. The department of economics decided to completely cut out part-time faculty in favour of hiring limited-term appointments (<span class="caps">LTA</span>s), which are lecturers who teach seven courses per year on contract for replacements or temporary positions. <span class="caps">LTA</span>s may get rehired year after year, replacing part-time faculty. </p>\n\n\t<p>According to Blais, the Faculty of Arts and Sciences has been the hardest hit by budget cuts, with the department of sociology and anthropology\u2019s course offers declining the most rapidly from 58 postings in the 2013-2014 academic year to 22 in 2014-2015. Statistics for the current academic year are not yet available.</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cWe\u2019re going strongly after some minimum amount of work that can be given to part-time faculty,\u201d said Blais. \u201cWe need some kind of guarantee by the university because we cannot tolerate that the situation get any worse.\u201d</p>\n\n\t<p>Blais could not comment publicly on the negotiations concerning monetary clauses or salary demands, because they have yet to be tabled.</p>\n\n\t<p><span class="caps">CUFA</span>, which represents full-time faculty at Concordia, had their collective agreement for 2015-2018 approved by the Board of Directors on Sept. 16, ending the shortest negotiations Stathopoulos has seen in his 30-year career at Concordia, lasting only eight months. \u201cThe norm used to be one or two years,\u201d he said.</p>\n\n\t<p><span class="caps">CUFA</span> members voted for the agreement with a 94 per cent majority, which Stathopoulos said he satisfied with. He mentioned that only certain sections of the agreement were opened during negotiations in order to make talks more efficient and come to an agreement earlier. </p>\n\n\t<p>Amendments to the agreement include a $400 professional development allowance for <span class="caps">LTA</span>s, a pay scale increase based on past year\u2019s inflation rates, as well as step increase in compensation to <span class="caps">LTA</span>s who teach upwards of four different classes per year.</p>\n\n\t<p>Stathopoulos attributes this effectiveness to a \u201cmore academic\u201d negotiating team and to Concordia President Alan Shepard\u2019s administration, which he said has changed the course of negotiations for the better. </p>\n\n\t<p>\u201c[With the previous administration] the answer to our questions used to be \u2018We don\u2019t have to tell you,\u2019\u201d he said. \u201cThis time we didn\u2019t have that attitude, they were very cooperative. </p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cI want to be optimistic that what worked for us will work for [CUPFA],\u201d Stathopoulos continued. \u201cThings are going in the right direction. The culture [of the previous administration] was negative and I think it takes a long time to fix that.\u201d</p>\n\n\t<p>Both faculty associations are awaiting pension reform negotiations.</p>\n\n\t<p>Concordia\u2019s spokesperson Chris Mota stated the university couldn\u2019t comment on bargaining agreements that are ongoing, but said that they \u201center all bargaining in good faith.\u201d</p>\n\t\t\t\n\n\t\n\t\n\t\n\t\n\t<!-- AddThis Button BEGIN -->\n<div class="addthis_toolbox addthis_default_style ">\n<a class="addthis_button_facebook_like"
