class="subhead">CUPFA Wants More Course Assignments, Administrative Work and Representation</h2>\n\t\n\t<p class="byline"><span class="section-badge">News</span> by <a href="/author/jonathan-cook">Jonathan Caragay-Cook</a> &amp; <a href="/author/kelsey-litwin">Kelsey Litwin</a> \u2014 Published October 18, 2016 | <em class="ss-icon ss-chat"></em><a class="comment-count" data-disqus-identifier="thelink_entry_9015" href="#disqus_thread">Comments</a></p>\n\t\n\t<p><a class="twitter-follow-button" data-dnt="true" data-show-count="false" data-show-screen-name="true" href="https://twitter.com/Hiimbiracial">Follow @Hiimbiracial</a><a class="twitter-follow-button" data-dnt="true" data-show-count="false" data-show-screen-name="true" href="https://twitter.com/kelseylitwin">Follow @kelseylitwin</a></p>\n\t\n\t<div class="entry-images full flexslider"><ul class="slides group">\n\t\n\t\t<li class="figure">\n\t\t\t<a class="cb" href="https://thelinknewspaper.ca/images/made/images/articles/Volume_37/News/_resized/8ca.cupfa(kelsey_litwin)1web_900_612_90.jpg" rel="images" title="David Douglas and Patrice Blais explained that they were not pleased with the negotiations. ">\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t<img alt="" src="/images/made/images/articles/Volume_37/News/_resized/8ca.cupfa(kelsey_litwin)1web_820_558_90.jpg"/>\n\t\t\t</a>\n\t\t\t<p>David Douglas and Patrice Blais explained that they were not pleased with the negotiations.  Photo Kelsey Litwin</p>\n\t\t</li>\n\t</ul></div>\n\t\n\t\n\t\t<div class="intro"><p>Five hundred and thirty-six days\u2014and counting.</p></div>\n\t\n\n\t\n\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\t<p>That\u2019s how long the Concordia University Part-Time Faculty Association has been without a collective agreement. </p>\n\n\t<p>On the homepage of CUPFA\u2019s site, there is a clock counting up the minutes since the union and the university last reached a retroactive agreement in March of 2015. It was only in effect for a month before the clock started ticking again.   </p>\n\n\t<p>The negotiating parties met on Friday, and there are six more dates scheduled from now until December. \u201cI\u2019m concerned at this point,\u201d said <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> President David Douglas. \u201cWe were not impressed with what happened over the summer.\u201d </p>\n\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t<blockquote class="pullquote">\n\t\t\t<p>\u201cI\u2019m concerned at this point. We were not impressed with what happened over the summer.\u201d <em>\u2014CUPFA President David Douglas</em></p>\n\t\t</blockquote>\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\t<p>\u201cThere are many reasons why an agreement hasn\u2019t been reached yet, with one of the main issues being the number of courses allotted to part-time faculty,\u201d said Patrice Blais, Vice-President Grievance and Collective Agreement. </p>\n\n\t<p>According to numbers from the university that <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> compiled, part-time faculty members have lost 10.8 per cent of available courses since the 2012-13 academic year. The number of courses overall has fallen 4.4 per cent, but full-time faculty have only seen a 0.1 per cent decrease in the same period. </p>\n\n\t<p>Beginning in the 2014-15 academic year, part-time faculty each received, including vacation pay, $8,327.88 for each three-credit course they taught. The amount of courses a part-time professor can teach is based on a seniority system. </p>\n\n\t<p>Professors accumulate seniority credits equivalent to the number of credits per course they taught\u2014teaching a three credit class equals three seniority credits. </p>\n\n\t<p>After reaching certain thresholds, these professors are eligible to teach more classes in one year. For example, part-time faculty with fewer than 24 credits of seniority can only be assigned six credits, if the courses are available. </p>\n\n\t<p>Part-time professors who have acquired 90 or more seniority credits are capable of teaching up to 18 credits, approximately six classes\u2014again, only if courses are available.</p>\n\n\t<p>The most compensation a part-time professor can earn is approximately $50,000 per year. But to reach the senior status of having 90 or more credits and be eligible to earn this amount, Blais estimates it can take anywhere from ten to 14 years. </p>\n\n\t<p>However, being assigned 18 credits actually becomes more probable around the 200 seniority credit-mark, he said. \u201cFor some departments, it\u2019s impossible,\u201d he added. </p>\n\n\t<p>The reason why part-time faculty is losing courses, they say, is because of the university favouring the hires of Limited Term Appointments. An <span class="caps">LTA</span> is a shortened contract\u2014maxing out at three years per renewable term with a mandatory two-year waiting period before reapplying\u2014that resides under the Concordia University Faculty Association. </p>\n\n\t<p>The <span class="caps">CUFA</span> (not <span class="caps">CUPFA</span>) collective agreement stipulates that <span class="caps">LTA</span> members with a 12-month contract must take on a minimum of seven courses per academic year. </p>\n\n\t<p>From an institutional standpoint, <span class="caps">LTA</span>s provide the university with a greater immediate value for money spent than part-time faculty members, Blais explained. He said: \u201cYou can pay them less and get more courses.\u201d </p>\n\n\t<p>An LTA\u2019s pay grade, despite the increased course load, is in a similar range to that of a part-time professor. An <span class="caps">LTA</span> lecturer whose contract started June 1, 2016 will have a salary of $57,734 for that first year, increasing to $60,960 in their second year. </p>\n\n\t<p>The only distinction in pay grade in regards to the number of courses being taught is a $1,500 course preparation stipend for each distinct course beyond five courses. </p>\n\n\t<p>This means that the amount is only awarded if a member is teaching five or more unique courses, and not multiple sections of the same course. This clause, which takes into account the additional work required of faculty members, is one that <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> does not have.</p>\n\n\t<p><span class="caps">CUPFA</span> argues that <span class="caps">LTA</span>s, as described in the <span class="caps">CUFA</span> Collective Agreement, are a temporary fix to the faculty shortage problem. </p>\n\n\t<p>The agreement specifies that those members can only be appointed in one of three situations: Replacing a faculty member on leave, \u201cfilling an open probationary position on a temporary basis,\u201d or temporarily teaching a course that requires specific knowledge and background. By filling in these positions with <span class="caps">LTA</span>s, they say, the university loses the experience that long-term part-time faculty members bring to the table. </p>\n\n\t<p>Douglas, president of <span class="caps">CUPFA</span>, says part-time professors add sustainability to the university. \u201cThere\u2019s institutional memory that comes with part-time [faculty].\u201d</p>\n\n\t<p><span class="caps">CUPFA</span> is also asking for increased remuneration. Blais wouldn\u2019t specify how much they\u2019re asking for but says they retracted their last offer in September after complications arose during negotiations. The negotiation team for Concordia has also changed since then, Blais said, adding that they have yet to meet the full team.</p>\n\n\t\n\t\t<div class="figure alignright">\n\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t<a class="cb" href="https://thelinknewspaper.ca/images/made/images/articles/Volume_37/News/_resized/8ca.cupfa(kelsey_litwin)2web_900_600_90.jpg" rel="images" title="New materials from CUPFA advocate for more work and recognition from Concordia. Photo Kelsey Litwin"><img alt="" src="/images/made/images/articles/Volume_37/News/_resized/8ca.cupfa(kelsey_litwin)2web_440_293_90.jpg"/></a>\t\t\n\t\t\t\n\t\t\t<p class="caption">New materials from CUPFA advocate for more work and recognition from Concordia. Photo Kelsey Litwin</p>\n\t\t</div>\n\t\t\n\n\t<p>On top of these concerns, Douglas says members of <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> want more administrative work, research opportunities, and representation on university bodies such as its pension plan committee. </p>\n\n\t<p>To meet these demands, the executive of <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> is already searching for community support. They plan to garner student solidarity through distributing mini-petition cards addressed to the university administration that read: \u201cI value part-time professors and support their right to undertake and be paid for administrative work on behalf of the students.\u201d Striking is a possibility, Douglas says, but not in the immediate future. </p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cWe can all come to the table and work for the best,\u201d said Douglas, \u201cor I can pull together the pressure and start making things uncomfortable. Those are the two options I have. I\u2019m hoping the university will see that the former is better than the latter\u2014if they don\u2019t, I\u2019ll use the latter.\u201d</p>\n\n\t<p>The university couldn\u2019t discuss the details of the ongoing negotiation process due to confidentiality, according to spokesperson Chris Mota.</p>\n\t\t\t\n\n\t\n\t\n\t\n\t\n\t<!-- AddThis Button BEGIN -->\n<div class="addthis_toolbox
