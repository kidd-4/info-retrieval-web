href="https://thelinknewspaper.ca/images/made/images/articles/Volume_36/News/_resized/5CA.GMBuilding(JonCook)_900_600_90.jpg" rel="images" title="The unions for Concordia\u2019s part-time faculty and File Photo Jonathan Caragay-Cook </p>\n\t\t</li>\n\t</ul></div>\n\t\n\t\n\t\t<div class="intro"><p>As the semester comes to a mid-point, two of Concordia\u2019s teaching unions remain out-of-contract, or without a salary agreement.</p></div>\n\t\n\n\t\n\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\t<p>Concordia University Part-time Faculty Association doesn\u2019t have a new collective agreement and is in the process of negotiating monetary issues with the university. Both sides have presented their offers as further bargaining steps will come next. <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> has been out of a contract since May 2015.</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cThere\u2019s some distance between where we are, but we\u2019re talking with them,\u201d said David Douglas, <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> President.</p>\n\n\t<p>Concordia President Alan Shepard said that negotiations are going well, and he\u2019s very hopeful that the university will reach an agreement with <span class="caps">CUPFA</span>.</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cThe issues that we have are significant and it will take some time to work our way through them,\u201d Douglas said. \u201cThere is good will.\u201d</p>\n\n\t<p><span class="caps">CUPFA</span> is demanding better salaries and course loads for part-time faculty, which have seen a reduction in the number of classes offered over the last few years.</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cWe have been the ones hit particularly hard by the retro budget cuts that Quebec threw at Concordia a couple years back,\u201d Douglas said.</p>\n\n\t<p>Since then, the budget cuts that followed have continued to affect part-time professors, he added. Changes are also coming for university employee pension plans that will increase the contribution ratio for employees to 50 per cent.</p>\n\n\t<p>The union for Teaching and Research Assistants at Concordia was supposed to renegotiate pay grades for the 2015-2016 year before May 2015. Talks were stalled in the fall after Concordia refused to offer teaching assistants a single pay rate for Undergraduate, Master\u2019s and PhD students based on their job description.</p>\n\n\t<p>TAs are central to academic institution, Shepard said, calling them \u201cvalued members of the team.\u201d He couldn\u2019t provide further comment on negotiations with <span class="caps">TRAC</span>, saying he didn\u2019t want to \u201ccomment on labour negotiations through the paper.\u201d</p>\n\n\t<p><span class="caps">TRAC</span> President Nader Jafar Nodoushan says the university is backtracking on an agreement to offer all TAs the same pay based on their level of education. This agreement was made during contract negotiations two years ago.</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cConcordia University will not accept the difference between Concordia\u2019s proposal and union\u2019s demand which was just around $360,000 for around 1,700 TAs, but they accept to pay one person $235,000 as a severance/compensation package,\u201d Nodoushan wrote in an open letter. </p>\n\n\t<p>His statement refers to a one-year salary severance package given to Concordia <span class="caps">CFO</span> Sonia Trudel, who was at the university for less than three months. Nodoushan says the amount <span class="caps">TRAC</span> is requesting to level pay rates will cost an additional $360,000 per year. Concordia President Alan Shepard\u2019s salary was $370,000 in 2015-2014.</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cLosing a senior administrator who has just been hired is not a good thing,\u201d Douglas said about the severance paid to Trudel. \u201cIt certainly raises a lot of questions about the hiring process.\u201d</p>\n\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t<blockquote class="pullquote">\n\t\t\t<p>\u201cLosing a senior administrator who has just been hired is not a good thing,\u201d <em>\u2014 David Douglas, <span class="caps">CUPFA</span> President</em> </p>\n\t\t</blockquote>\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t\t<p>The university has not provided explanation about the CFO\u2019s termination, except that it was \u201camicable.\u201d</p>\n\n\t<p>Douglas says he isn\u2019t surprised with the severance package, but doesn\u2019t agree with the amount given to Trudel. </p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cTo find someone who\u2019s capable of filling the <span class="caps">CFO</span> position, you need someone who can operate on a budget of scale,\u201d Douglas said. \u201cThat\u2019s not everybody, but I\u2019m not necessarily convinced that you need someone who could do the same thing in the private sector.\u201d</p>\n\n\t<p>The roles and perks of being a business executive versus a university senior admin don\u2019t compare, because they are operating in different economies, Douglas said.</p>\n\n\t<p>\u201cWe\u2019re not the private sector, we\u2019re the public sector.\u201d</p>\n\t\t\t\n\n\t\n\t\n\t\n\t\n\t<!-- AddThis Button BEGIN
